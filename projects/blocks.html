Overview

One system, many instruments

BLOCKS is a modular controller designed to open up the world of music-making to everyone. The system has five Blocks: the Lightpad Block (a tactile, glowing surface), the Seaboard Block (a touch-responsive keyboard with 24 keywaves), and three Control Blocks covering different phases of the music-creation process. They connect magnetically into custom instruments that suit any budget, skill level, and style.

The pages that follow document my personal work on the project.

Research

Less intimidating, not less powerful

BLOCKS began with a simple question: how do you make music creation less intimidating without making it less powerful?

Starting from a blank sheet, we analysed existing controllers, production software, and musical workflows to identify the essential building blocks of music-making. Rather than design a single instrument, we decomposed the process into smaller interaction primitives and explored how they could be combined physically. Many of those early explorations eventually became the three Control Blocks, each optimised around a different style of creation and performance.

Prototyping

Finding where the interaction breaks

Over three years I built and tested dozens of interaction concepts with musicians. Some explored obvious extensions — physical controls for mixing and performance. Others investigated less conventional ideas: rotary surfaces, haptic feedback, gesture-driven inputs, and alternative approaches to musical control.

The goal was rarely to validate a solution. More often it was to understand where the interaction broke down, what musicians do instinctively, and which behaviours felt expressive enough to become part of the Roli ecosystem.

CMF

The surface that disappears

One challenge consumed far more time than expected: the elastomer playing surface. The goal was a surface that looked completely black and inactive when powered off, yet revealed a vibrant, illuminated interface the moment it was in use.

To get there I experimented with coatings, laser-etched finishes, and material combinations on transparent silicone. The final solution became a defining characteristic of the product — letting the interface disappear until exactly the moment it was needed.
